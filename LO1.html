üß™ Website & Gameplay Design

Once we officially started designing our website, my group and I began brainstorming what it should include. We grabbed a whiteboard (like in the photo) and started sketching and discussing.
We first defined our main goals for the project:
‚Ä¢ Raise awareness about dyslexia
‚Ä¢ Simulate how it feels to live with the condition
‚Ä¢ Include a game that shows the challenges dyslexic people face
‚Ä¢ Present multiple types of dyslexia, not just one

After deciding on six main pages for the site, we worked on improving each one and checking if it made sense, if it was fun, or if it was too boring.

For the gameplay page, I suggested adding levels: starting with easier, shorter texts and increasing difficulty to show different dyslexia types. I also had the idea of timing users while they read a normal paragraph, then comparing it with a dyslexia-effect version, to show how reading speed changes. This would help raise awareness, especially about how dyslexic kids are often bullied for reading slowly. Unfortunately, my teammates felt this made it too much like a game, so those ideas didn‚Äôt make it in.

We spent some time discussing the layout for the detailed info page, whether to go horizontal or vertical, and where the dyslexia effect toggle should be.

For the victory page, I suggested a point system and a timer to make it feel more rewarding.
Once we finalized the page structure, we divided the prototyping tasks. I chose the game setup and victory pages. I thought we should continue on using the same color theme as our poster to keep everything consistent.

At first, for the game setup, I had a big info paragraph on one side and three questions on the other, but it felt cramped and uninviting.
So, I changed it: each paragraph became its own question, keeping the focus on educating users about dyslexia while making it interactive. I also tried using different answer types like multiple choice, fill-in-the-blank, etc. but the group wanted to keep things simple, so we stuck with one-answer options only.

After our feedback session with Chris, I wanted to make the game side more fun to keep users engaged. I explored simple game designs, thought of Chris‚Äôs idea for FlexBox Froggy, checked out other websites and came up with a spaceship path idea, where each dot represented a difficulty level. But again, the team felt this was ‚Äútoo gamified.‚Äù
For the victory page, I kept it light: I first thought of adding fireworks or confetti. Then Adeline made a design with scattered letters, which inspired me to swap the confetti with falling letters to stay on theme. This is still a goal for the future because we felt like it would be too much letters.
After mostly finalizing my pages, I decided to do some user testing. I created a few layout options for the gameplay page, thinking about what users might prefer. I tried having each paragraph linked to its own question, either arranged horizontally or vertically, showing them individually on the screen. I also tested a completely different version with funny, super simple text so users wouldn't get bored or feel like they were doing schoolwork. I asked Pennie to user test it as well, she didnt like how the original layout looked(the full text and all questions being next to each other) - "its too busy and overwhelming. i feel like im getting a headache, and is tricking my eyes"

After testing, it came down to two options: vertical text with each section and question together, or showing them one at a time on separate screens. In my opinion, having everything individually on the screen left too much empty space. If we filled the space with design, it became too crowded and hard to focus. But if we left it as-is, it felt too simple, boring, and kind of unprofessional. So i stuck with the first option instead. This process helped me understand real UX preferences and the importance of balance between playfulness and professionalism.

Reflection: This whole planning phase taught me how important it is to combine creativity with what works. I came in with a bunch of ideas that I was really excited about, like using levels or comparing reading speed with and without dyslexia effects. I still think those concepts had a lot of potential to make the experience more meaningful, but I learned that not every idea fits the group's vision. It was a bit frustrating at times, but it helped me understand how to compromise and prioritize the bigger picture. Seeing our ideas come to life on the whiteboard felt like real progress. It was cool to physically map things out, make changes on the spot, and hear everyone's input. I also learned a lot from the user testing I did later‚Äîsometimes what makes sense in your head doesn‚Äôt always feel right to others. I kept adjusting layouts and content to find the best balance between education, interaction, and simplicity. In the end, even though not all of my ideas made it in, I‚Äôm proud of how much I contributed and how much the process taught me about team communication, flexibility, and design thinking.

üß† User Testing & Layout Iteration

When we began designing the gameplay section, I knew the layout would play a big role in how users experienced the content.
So, I created and tested three different layout styles:
‚Ä¢ A horizontal layout where each paragraph and its question appeared side by side
‚Ä¢ A vertical layout with all elements stacked top to bottom
‚Ä¢ A single-question-per-screen layout, where users saw only one section at a time


Through testing, it became clear which worked and which didn‚Äôt.
The vertical layout was difficult to follow, and the single-screen version felt slow and overly empty. some even compared it to boring school slides.
In contrast, the horizontal layout felt much clearer. It allowed users to scroll through smoothly, keeping everything in one place without feeling cluttered.
During a user testing session, classmates gave helpful feedback.
Most preferred the horizontal layout, saying it was easier to understand and more natural to follow.
Pennie mentioned that the vertical version gave her a headache, while the horizontal one felt more intuitive.
Dirk was the only one who liked the one-question-per-screen layout‚Äîhe thought it might help users stay focused.

Reflection

This testing process showed me how drastically layout can impact the user experience. Even small design decisions can change how people feel about a product. It also reminded me that your first idea isn‚Äôt always the best‚Äîearly testing and real feedback are essential for making smart design choices.
